Relative Clauses

Relative clauses give us more information about someone or something. We can use relative clauses to combine clauses without repeating information.

Example:

Original: The couple sent a Christmas present to their daughter. Their daughter lives in America.

Relative Clause: The couple sent a Christmas present to their daughter, who lives in America.


Defining Relative Clauses

We use defining relative clauses to give essential information about someone or something. This is information that we need in order to understand what or who is being referred to. A defining relative clause usually comes immediately after the noun it describes.

We usually use a relative pronoun (who, that, which, whose and whom) to introduce a defining relative clause.

Examples:

They are the people who want to buy our house. (In this sentences the defining relative clause is: who want to buy our house. It refers to the people).

Here are some cells which have not been affected. (In this sentences the defining relative clause is: which have not been affected. It refers to some cells).


IMPORTANT

We DO NOT use commas in defining relative clauses.


Non-Defining Relative Clauses

We use non-defining relative clauses to give extra information about the person or thing. It is not necessary information. We don’t need it to understand who or what is being referred to.

We always use a relative pronoun (who, which, whose or whom) to introduce a non-defining relative clause.

Examples:

Jimmy, who I work with, is doing the London Marathon this year. (In this sentences the non-defining relative clause is: who I work with. It refers to Jimmy).

Roger Federer, who has won 20 Grand Slam titles, does not play tennis anymore. (In this sentences the non-defining relative clause is: who has won 20 Grand Slam titles. It refers to Roger Federer).


IMPORTANT

We DO NOT use the relative pronoun THAT to introduce a non-defining relative clause.

We NEED commas around the non-defining relative clause.


Defining vs. Non-Defining Relative Clauses

Sometimes defining and non-defining relative clauses can look very similar but have different meanings.

Examples:

Defining: His brother who works at the supermarket is a friend of mine. (In this sentence, the person has more than one brother. The one I’m talking about works at the supermarket.)

Non-Defining: His brother, who works at the supermarket, is a friend of mine. (In this sentence, the person only has one brother, and that brother works at the supermarket.)


IMPORTANT

The information in a defining relative clause is essential, so we can’t leave out the relative clause.

The information in a non-defining relative clause is extra information which isn’t essential, so we can leave out the relative clause.
