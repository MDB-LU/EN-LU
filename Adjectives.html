Adjectives

Adjectives are words that describe a noun or a pronoun.

Examples:

My sister is happy. (The adjective happy describes the noun sister.)

He looks tired. (The adjective tired describes the pronoun he.)


Comparative

If we compare two things, we have to use the comparative form of the adjective.


If the adjective only has one syllable, we can add the ending er.

Example:

My brother is stronger than you.


If the adjective has two syllables but ends with the letter y, we have to change the y into an i before adding the ending er.

Examples:

My sister is funnier than your brother.

My room is dirtier than yours.


If the adjective has two or more syllables, the adjective stays the same, but we have to add the word more before the adjective.

Examples:

My shirt was more expensive than yours.

New York is more dangerous than Berlin.


IMPORTANT

When we compare two things with each other, we have to use the comparative and the word than.

Example:

His friend is taller than you.


Superlative

We use the superlative form of the adjective to describe something as being of the highest degree.


If the adjective only has one syllable, we can add the ending est.

Example:

My brother is the strongest man in the world.


If the adjective has two syllables but ends with the letter y, we have to change the y into an i before adding the ending est.

Examples:

My sister is the funniest person in the room.

My room is the dirtiest in the house.


If the adjective has two or more syllables, the adjective stays the same, but we have to add the word most before the adjective.

Examples:

My shirt was the most expensive shirt I have ever bought.

New York is the most dangerous city on earth.


IMPORTANT

When we use the superlative, we always have to use the article the before the superlative form.

Examples:

This picture is the cheapest in this exhibition.

Mary is the most careful person.


Irregular Adjectives

Just like in German, there are some irregular adjectives. Here are some of the most common examples:

Good, better, best

Bad, worse, worst

Far, further, furthest


Adjectives ending in -ed or -ing:

Some adjectives can have two endings. For example boring and bored. But when do we use which one?


Adjectives ending in -ing describe a situation, a person or a thing.

Examples:

This is so exciting.

My brother is really annoying.

This film is boring.


Adjectives ending in -ed describe how people feel.

Examples:

The students are bored.

John is very excited.
