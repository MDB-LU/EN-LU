Adverbs

An adverb is a word that describes a verb, an adjective, another adverb or even a whole clause.

Examples:

He sings loudly. (The adverb loudly describes the verb sings.)

She is very tall. (The adverb very describes the adjective tall.)

The presentation ended too quickly. (The adverb too describes another adverb: quickly.)

Fortunately, I had brought an umbrella. (The adverb fortunately describes the whole clause.)


Adverbs - Endings

Adverbs often end in -ly:

loudly, slowly, happily, quickly, badly, really


But there are also adverbs, that look the same as the adjective:

fast, far, long, early


Adverbs - Placement

In general, adverbs should be placed as close as possible to the words they describe. Placing the adverb in the wrong spot could completely change the meaning of the sentence. Consider the difference in meaning between the following two sentences:

1. I almost dropped all the plates I was holding.

2. I dropped almost all the plates I was holding.

In the first sentence, the person did not drop the plates. Nothing fell to the ground. In the second sentence, the person did drop the plates, but not all of them. This shows you that it is really important where you place the adjective.


Adverbs - Types

Adverbs have different meanings and functions. They are especially important for indicating the time, manner, place, degree and frequency of something.

Example for time: I never get up early at the weekends.

Example for manner: Walk across the road carefully!

Example for place: When we got there, they had already left.

Example for degree: It is rather cold, isn't it?

Example for frequency: I'm always late for work.
